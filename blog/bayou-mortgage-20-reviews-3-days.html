They're the first thing a future customer looks at, the strongest signal Google uses to decide who shows up in the map pack, and the single biggest factor in whether someone calls you or your competitor. No paid incentives. No fake accounts. No review-gating tricks. Just a simple, intentional system that turned existing happy clients into public advocates over a single weekend.

This is the same playbook we now run for every client we onboard at Multiply Local — and it works in any local industry where you have past customers sitting in a CRM, an inbox, or a phone contact list.

The Starting Point: 4 Reviews and Invisible

When we first sat down with the team at Bayou Mortgage, the situation was familiar. They were doing great work — closing loans, helping families into homes, and getting referrals from happy clients — but their Google Business Profile told a completely different story. Four reviews. A 4.5 star rating. Buried under three other lenders in the local map pack.

That gap between reality and online reputation is where most local businesses are quietly losing money every month. The work is great. The customers are happy. But Google has no idea, because nobody is being asked to share what they thought.

We knew the demand was there. We knew the trust was there. We just needed to give the existing client base an easy way to say so out loud.

Why This Matters

Going from 4 reviews to 24 reviews in 72 hours doesn't just look better — it fundamentally changes how Google ranks the business and how a future customer perceives it. A jump like that pushes a profile from "I guess they're fine" to "this is clearly the obvious choice."

The Strategy: Intentional Outreach to Real Clients

Most businesses think "review generation" means installing a gimmick — a QR code on the counter, a kiosk in the lobby, a generic "leave us a review!" email blast that everyone ignores. None of those work. They feel impersonal, they're easy to dismiss, and the people who respond to them are usually the ones with a complaint, not praise.

Our approach for Bayou Mortgage was the opposite. Personal. Intentional. Direct. The plan came down to four simple ideas:

The 4-Part System

1

Build a Curated List of Past Clients

We didn't blast everyone in the database. We worked with the Bayou Mortgage team to pull a list of clients from the last 12 months whose loans had closed cleanly — people we knew had a positive experience and who would remember the name. About 60 names made the cut. That focused list was the foundation of the entire push.

2

Send a Personalized Text — Not an Email

Email open rates for review requests average around 20%. Text message open rates sit above 95%, and most people read them within three minutes. We wrote a short, personal text from the loan officer who actually worked with the client, mentioning their name and a specific detail from their loan, and ending with a direct one-tap link to the Google review form. No "click here to rate us" — just a real message from a real person.

3

Send in Small Waves — Not All at Once

We split the list into three waves of about 20 people each, sent over three days. Why? Two reasons. First, Google flags suspicious review velocity — if 60 reviews appear on a profile in one hour, the system gets suspicious. Second, spreading the requests gave the loan officers time to personally respond to every reviewer the same day. That follow-up step is what turned a one-time push into a long-term reputation system.

4

Reply to Every Single Review the Same Day

Within hours of each review going live, the loan officer replied — by name, referencing a specific moment from the loan process. "Thanks Sarah — so glad we got the closing across the finish line before your move-in date!" Google has confirmed that response rate is a ranking factor, and from a customer perspective it signals that the business actually pays attention. This step alone separates Bayou Mortgage from 90% of local lenders.

The Results: 20 Reviews, 72 Hours

By the end of day three, Bayou Mortgage had logged 20 brand-new five-star reviews on their Google Business Profile. The overall rating jumped from 4.5 to a clean 5.0. The total review count went from 4 to 24 — a 6x increase in three days.

But the headline numbers weren't even the most important part. Here's what else changed in those three days:

Map Pack Visibility

Within 48 hours of the review push, Bayou Mortgage started showing up in the local map pack for "mortgage lender near me" and similar searches in their service area — searches they had been completely invisible for the week before. Google's local algorithm rewards review volume, recency, and rating, and the system reacted almost immediately.

Profile Engagement

Profile views, direction requests, and click-to-call actions on the Google Business Profile all spiked in the days following the push. More reviews don't just rank you higher — they make people who find you more likely to actually engage. Trust translates directly into action.

A Repeatable Engine

The biggest win wasn't the 20 reviews. It was the system. Bayou Mortgage now has a process: every closed loan triggers a personal text request 24 hours after closing, every reviewer gets a same-day reply, and the team has a template they can run forever. Reviews don't come in 20-at-a-time anymore — they come in steadily, every single week, on autopilot.

The Compounding Effect

One review push gets you a moment. A repeatable review system gets you a moat. Three months in, Bayou Mortgage is now sitting at 40+ reviews and has the highest rating of any independent lender in their market. That gap will only widen — because the competition still isn't asking.

Why This System Works (And Most Don't)

The reason most review generation campaigns fail isn't because customers don't want to leave reviews — it's because the ask is wrong. Here are the four things this system gets right that most businesses get wrong.

It's Personal, Not Automated-Feeling

A text from "Channing at Bayou Mortgage" lands completely differently than an email from "Bayou Mortgage Customer Service." The personal touch is what gets the response. Every message looked like a one-off from a real person, because in spirit, it was.

It's Easy

The link in the message dropped people directly into Google's review form on their phone, pre-loaded with the Bayou Mortgage profile. No searching. No scrolling. No logging in. One tap, five stars, 30 seconds. Friction kills review rates more than anything else, and we eliminated all of it.

It's Asked at the Right Time

The clients we contacted had closed loans recently enough to remember the experience clearly, but long enough ago that they were past the stress of the closing itself. That sweet spot — usually 2 to 8 weeks after the transaction — is when people are most likely to write a thoughtful, positive review.

It Closes the Loop

Most businesses ask for the review and then disappear. The reply step matters as much as the ask. When a customer leaves a review and gets a personal response within hours, two things happen: they feel valued, and Google notices the engagement. Both feed the next review and the next ranking boost.

How to Copy This for Your Business

If you run a local service business — plumbing, HVAC, roofing, landscaping, contracting, real estate, anything where past customers exist — you can run this exact playbook this weekend. Here's the short version:

  1. Pull your list. Go into your CRM, your invoicing tool, or your phone contacts and find every happy customer from the last 12 months. Don't overthink it. Aim for 30 to 60 names.
  2. Get your Google review link. In Google Business Profile, click "Share review form" and copy the short link. This is the link you'll send.
  3. Write a personal text template. Keep it short. Use the customer's first name. Reference a specific detail. End with the link. Sample: "Hey [Name] — this is [Your Name] over at [Business]. Just wanted to say thanks again for trusting us with [the project]. If you've got 30 seconds and would be open to leaving us a quick Google review, it would mean a ton. Here's the link: [link]."
  4. Send in waves. Don't blast all 60 at once. Send 15-20 per day across 3-4 days. Spread it out.
  5. Reply to every review the same day. Personal. By name. Reference something specific. This is non-negotiable.
  6. Make it ongoing. Build the request into the natural end of every job. One review at a time, forever.
A Word of Warning

Do not offer money, discounts, or anything else in exchange for reviews. It's against Google's terms of service and it can get your profile suspended. Don't review-gate either (only sending happy customers to Google while diverting unhappy ones to a private form). Stay clean. The system works because it's honest.

Frequently Asked Questions

How did you actually get 20 Google reviews in 3 days?

We built a simple review request system: a curated list of past clients, a personalized text message with a direct review link, sent in small batches over three days, with a same-day thank-you reply to every reviewer. No incentives, no automation gimmicks — just intentional outreach to people who already loved working with Bayou Mortgage.

Is asking customers for reviews against Google's policy?

No. Google explicitly allows businesses to ask customers for honest reviews. What's not allowed is offering incentives in exchange for reviews, soliciting reviews from non-customers, or review-gating (only sending happy customers to Google while sending unhappy ones to a private feedback form). Our system stays fully compliant.

Why do Google reviews matter so much for a mortgage company?

Mortgage is a high-trust, high-stakes purchase. Most borrowers compare two or three lenders before choosing, and the one with stronger reviews almost always wins. Reviews also feed Google's local ranking algorithm — more reviews and higher ratings push you up in the local map pack, where most discovery happens.

Will this same system work for my local business?

Yes — and in most cases even faster. Bayou Mortgage's customer base is smaller than a typical home service business. Plumbers, HVAC companies, roofers, landscapers, and contractors usually have hundreds of past customers sitting in a CRM or invoicing tool. Most of them would gladly leave a review if asked the right way.
